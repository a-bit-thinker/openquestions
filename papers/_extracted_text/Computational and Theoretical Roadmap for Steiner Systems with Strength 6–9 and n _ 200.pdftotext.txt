Computational and Theoretical Roadmap for
Steiner Systems with Strength 6–9 and n < 200
Executive summary
This report concerns Steiner systems in the modern sense: an S(r, q, n) is a collection of q -subsets
(“blocks”) of [n] such that every r -subset of [n] is contained in exactly one block. Your parameter regime is
n > q > r > 5, r ∈ {6, 7, 8, 9}, n < 200. This is the same as a r-(n, q, 1) design.       1




Two modern asymptotic existence theorems dominate the theoretical landscape. The randomized-algebraic
method of Peter Keevash 2 proves that, for fixed r, q , the standard divisibility conditions are sufficient for
all sufficiently large n, but the proof is nonconstructive in the small-n range and does not provide a
practical “n ≤ 200” threshold.     3   A second approach, the iterative-absorption proof of Stefan Glock         4

and collaborators, solves the more general F -decomposition problem and provides an alternative existence
proof for designs via absorption; it is likewise asymptotic.   5




For n < 200, the primary actionable constraints are (i) admissibility (binomial/divisibility conditions), plus (ii)
strong necessary lower bounds on n in terms of (r, q), and (iii) a small set of proven nonexistence results
that eliminate the very smallest admissible cases. In particular, Patric R. J. Östergård 6 and Olli Pottonen
  7  proved S(4, 5, 17) does not exist and (critically) this rules out all S(t, t + 1, t + 13) for t ≥ 4; this
directly eliminates (r, q, n) = (6, 7, 19), (7, 8, 20), (8, 9, 21), (9, 10, 22) in your range.   8   Moreover, J. G.
van der Pol   9   records additional nonexistences in the “q = r + 1” line, including S(5, 6, 16), which by
repeated derivation eliminates (6, 7, 17), (7, 8, 18), (8, 9, 19), (9, 10, 20).    10




Applying the standard divisibility conditions (for i = 0, … , r − 1) yields 254 admissible (r, q, n) triples

Existence theory and construction paradigms

Keevash’s theorem and the asymptotic–exact gap

Keevash proves the long-standing “existence conjecture for designs”: for fixed r, q , the divisibility conditions
are sufficient for the existence of r-(n, q, 1) designs for all sufficiently large n. The argument is framed
more generally as clique decompositions in hypergraphs/simplicial complexes under pseudorandomness
and extendability hypotheses, and it is nonconstructive at the scale of n < 200 (no explicit small thresholds
are provided in the theorem’s general form).        16




For your problem, the key implication is methodological rather than directly computational: admissibility is
believed to be “nearly sufficient”, but small-n behavior remains dominated by sporadic obstructions and
the absence of effective thresholds. This is exactly why n < 200 searches require a blend of (i) tight pruning
and (ii) heavy computational backtracking / SAT / ILP.    17




Iterative absorption and hypergraph decomposition

The iterative-absorption theorem of Glock and collaborators establishes existence of decompositions of
complete r -uniform hypergraphs (and much more general objects) into copies of a fixed r -uniform
hypergraph F , assuming divisibility conditions and sufficiently large size. In the special case where F is a
complete r -graph, this yields (another proof of) existence of block designs, driven by a probabilistic
“absorption” mechanism.        5




Practically, iterative absorption suggests algorithmic heuristics for construction-by-stages (build an almost-
design, then absorb leftover constraints). However, turning the existence proof into an efficient
implementation for n < 200 remains nontrivial; absorption methods are most useful as inspiration for
hybrid search (heuristic near-solutions + exact completion).   18

Kramer–Mesner orbit method and prescribed automorphism groups

The Kramer–Mesner method (often used as “orbit reduction”) is a computational framework for
constructing t-designs with a prescribed automorphism group G. One computes the orbits of G on r -
subsets (rows) and q -subsets (columns), forms the Kramer–Mesner incidence matrix A between orbit
representatives, and solves


                                                   Ax = 1

over x ∈ {0, 1}#(column orbits) , where selecting a column-orbit corresponds to including all blocks in that
orbit. The method originates in the 1976 paper of Earl S. Kramer 19 and Dale M. Mesner 20 .       21




Modern, practical support exists in GAP tooling (notably the PAG package manual explicitly documents
Kramer–Mesner style search workflows and related routines). 22 For n < 200, Kramer–Mesner is often
essential: without symmetry, the raw exact cover instance can easily exceed memory/time, while a good
choice of G can reduce the variable count by orders of magnitude.


Recursive constructions, finite geometry, and difference methods

Recursive constructions in design theory (e.g., via group-divisible designs and Wilson-type “fundamental
construction” ideas) are powerful for generating infinite families of designs, but typically with λ > 1 or with
parameters not matching Steiner λ = 1 at t ≥ 6. A key historical fact is that nontrivial t-designs exist for all
t (Teirlinck), but these constructions generally produce very large λ and are not tuned for Steiner λ = 1.
 15 A more recent example of a general recursive design-construction framework is the paper of Tran van
Trung 23 , which constructs simple t-designs for arbitrary t from ingredient designs by solving index
equations.   24




Finite geometry supplies many Steiner systems at small strength (notably t = 2, 3) and supplies high-
symmetry templates (affine/projective planes, nets, etc.), which can still be useful in your regime through
group choices for Kramer–Mesner and through “local geometry” heuristics. 25 Difference methods
(difference sets/families, cyclic development) are especially useful as symmetry constraints: one searches
for base blocks in a cyclic group and develops them to form a full design, converting the problem into
difference covering constraints. Although this is most developed for t = 2 (e.g., cyclic S(2, 4, v) surveys),
the methodology (“assume a regular cyclic action; search base blocks; lift to full block set”) generalizes as a
computational heuristic for t > 2. 26

Algorithmic search strategies and pruning heuristics
This section treats construction as a constraint satisfaction / exact cover family of problems and lays out
implementable search pipelines for n < 200.


Kramer–Mesner orbit reduction (linear algebra / 0–1 feasibility)

Core idea. Prescribe a permutation group G ≤ Sn acting on [n]. Let Or be the set of G-orbits on r -
                                                                                    ∣Or ∣×∣Oq ∣
subsets, and Oq the orbits on q -subsets. Form the orbit-incidence matrix A ∈ Z≥0                 , where Aij counts
how many r -subsets in row-orbit i occur inside a representative block of column-orbit j . Solve Ax = 1 in
x ∈ {0, 1}∣Oq ∣ ; each solution lifts to a G-invariant design.   21




Complexity profile. - Orbit generation dominates when n grows: worst case behaves like enumerating
subsets, but with strong G it can drop sharply. - Solving Ax = 1 is an NP-complete 0–1 feasibility problem
in general; however, ∣Oq ∣ can become small enough for SAT/ILP/DLX solvers.    32




Implementation notes. - Use GAP to define G, compute orbits, and assemble A; the PAG package is
explicitly designed for “prescribed automorphism group” workflows and includes Kramer–Mesner search
routines. 22 - Choose G from a menu of “likely” symmetry groups: cyclic Cn , dihedral D2n , affine groups
AGL(1, p) when n is prime, small transitive groups, or subgroup chains that allow incremental symmetry
breaking (start large, relax if no solutions). The usefulness of group actions for constructing high-t designs
is a standard theme in the computational symmetry literature.         33




Feasible parameter ranges (heuristic). - Without symmetry: orbit reduction gives no advantage. - With
moderate symmetry (say ∣G∣ ≳ 103 and few orbits), you can sometimes handle n up to 100–200 for q close
to r , because the orbit matrix becomes small even when the raw design is huge. This is precisely why
Kramer–Mesner remains relevant in high-t work.        34




Exact cover / DLX / libexact (with and without symmetry)

Core encoding. Create a constraint row for each r -subset of [n]. Create a variable/choice column for each
q -subset (“candidate block”). Put a 1 when the r-subset is contained in the block. Then an S(r, q, n) is
exactly a set of blocks that covers every row exactly once—an exact cover instance. This framing is
standard and is directly used in the proof-of-nonexistence search for S(4, 5, 17). 35


Algorithm DLX. Knuth’s “Dancing Links” (Algorithm X implemented with a sparse linked structure) is a
canonical exact cover enumerator; it uses reversible delete/undelete operations and typically branches on
the currently “smallest” constraint. 36 The libexact library provides an engineered exact cover solver
infrastructure. 37

Complexity profile. - Exponential time in the worst case (typical for exact cover and 0–1 CSP). 38 - Memory
is proportional to the number of 1s in the incidence matrix when using DLX-style linked nodes; this is a
practical limiter for n even when the solver is fast on pruned instances. 38

design as a bipartite incidence graph (points–blocks) or as a block-intersection graph, then apply canonical
labeling / automorphism computation via nauty/Traces. 39 - In practice, most DLX implementations
benefit from a “seed-and-extend” approach: fix a small configuration (one or several blocks), solve the
residual exact cover, and canonicalize seeds to avoid duplicates—this is exactly the structure of the
S(4, 5, 17) nonexistence computation (“derived designs” + exhaustive exact cover). 40

Heuristics and metaheuristics (hill climbing, local repair, absorption-inspired)

Why use them. For r ≥ 6, exact search can stall early. A useful intermediate objective is to construct a
partial block set that nearly covers each r -subset exactly once (minimizing squared deviations), then switch
to exact completion. This mirrors the “almost decomposition + absorption” philosophy in modern proofs.
 50




Evidence base. Metaheuristics have been used historically for lower-t Steiner systems (e.g., genetic
algorithms for Steiner triple systems), suggesting that heuristic seed generation can be effective even if the
final certification requires exact solvers. 51


Parallel / distributed search

Core approach. Branch the search by fixing a seed (or a sequence of orbit choices) and farm subinstances
to workers. This is natural for DLX and for SAT via assumptions. Distributed exact-cover architectures built
around DLX-style branching are documented (e.g., systems designed explicitly to distribute Knuth-style DLX
over many clients). 52


Implementation notes (robust pattern). - Choose a canonical seed depth d s.t. the number of
subinstances is large but each is manageable. - Save seeds and progress checkpoints; record unsat cores
(SAT) or infeasibility certificates (MIP) when possible. - Use a canonicalization step at every seed (nauty/
Traces) to prevent duplicated isomorphic subtrees. 53

High-priority “group menu” for Kramer–Mesner attempts

Because highly symmetric Steiner t-designs become rare at large t (and are strongly constrained in block-
transitive regimes), group choices should be pragmatic: aim for moderate reductions, not maximal
symmetry. 68 Suggested starting menu:


      • cyclic Cn and dihedral D2n (difference-method inspired),
      • small transitive groups of degree n (via GAP’s transitive group library),
      • affine groups when n has suitable arithmetic form (e.g., n = p prime),
      • subgroup chains G0 ≥ G1 ≥ … to gradually relax symmetry if no solution appears.       69



This strategy aligns with the “prescribed automorphism group” philosophy documented in the PAG manual
and in the Kramer–Mesner lineage. 43
